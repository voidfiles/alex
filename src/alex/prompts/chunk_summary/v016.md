Before writing, work carefully through the following. Treat this as a checklist for extraction, not as text to reproduce:
- What are the core arguments, claims, and conclusions actually stated in this section?
- What specific evidence, examples, data points, numbers, definitions, and citations appear, and exactly what does each one say?
- Which named author and year is attached to each claim, and what precisely does that source say (and not say)?
- What technical terms or concepts are introduced, and how are they precisely defined here?
- What concrete examples, anecdotes, analogies, lists, names, brand names, websites, institutions, or numeric figures appear—even if they seem incidental?
- What distinctions, qualifications, hedges, or caveats does the section itself make (e.g., what a term does NOT mean, that a study was never published, that a figure is statistically irrelevant, that a source did NOT claim something)?
- How does this section fit within the broader document structure?
- What does the section explicitly acknowledge as an assumption or limitation?

Two principles govern your summary. Both are equally important.

STRICT FIDELITY TO THE SOURCE. Every claim in your summary must be directly and literally supported by the text of THIS section. Do not infer, extrapolate, synthesize across the document, or characterize beyond what the text states. Specifically:
- Do not invent framing, labels, or "central contributions" the authors do not use. Do not describe a section as "synthesizing" or "integrating" things unless the text itself says so.
- Do not make claims about the document as a whole—its overall thesis, its number of steps or principles, the span of its reference list, its audience, its publication date, the balance of its examples—unless that information is explicitly present in THIS section's content. If this section is one chunk, summarize only that chunk; do not reconstruct the whole book.
- Attribute every citation, quotation, definition, and finding to the EXACT source named in the text. Do not merge two sources into one joint claim. If quote A comes from author X and quote B from author Y, keep them separate and attributed correctly. If a claim has no citation in this section, do not attach one.
- Represent each source precisely, including negative claims (what a source did not state, did not include, or did not measure) and status qualifiers (e.g., that a study was never published, is widely cited, or is statistically irrelevant).
- Do not assert that the text "acknowledges a difficulty," "concedes," or "candidly admits" something unless the text explicitly does so in those terms. Report the author's actual stance, including when the author dismisses or rejects an idea.
- If something is ambiguous or only implied, either omit it or mark it explicitly as your interpretation rather than asserting it as fact.

COMPLETE AND SPECIFIC COVERAGE. Capture the section's full substance, not just its high-level thrust. Omitting concrete, salient details is a failure even when the gist is correct. You must capture:
- Every named author and the specific year of each cited work, with the precise claim attributed to each (e.g., a finding about intentions versus behaviour attributed to its named authors and years).
- Exact definitions, the precise wording of distinctions (e.g., between two named components or concepts), and any enumerated categories, lists, or steps—reproduce list items in full rather than summarizing them as "various categories."
- Concrete examples, anecdotes, and analogies in their specifics, including proper nouns: named people, named studies, named tools or brands, named institutions, named websites or URLs, and the like.
- Numeric figures and quantities exactly as stated (percentages, time estimates, counts, thresholds), together with any qualification the text attaches to them.
- Fine-grained but important factual and terminological points, including historical observations and notes about what was or was not the case in an original formulation.
- Any media references, headlines, or titles quoted in the text, reproduced accurately.
- The section's own identification of what it is, when stated (for example, that the section is a bibliography, an index, a list of categories, or an afterword)—and in such cases, faithfully record the representative entries it actually contains rather than glossing over them.

Now write a summary that:

1. Captures core arguments: Identify the central claims and supporting points of this section with precision, in the section's own terms.
2. Reports evidence and methodology faithfully: Explain how the points are supported, naming each source and the specific claim it backs, keeping separate sources separate.
3. Preserves key definitions and distinctions: Record technical terms, their exact definitions, and any explicit contrasts, hedges, or qualifications.
4. Retains salient specifics: Include named authors, years, examples, anecdotes, analogies, enumerated lists (in full), numbers, brand and site names, headlines, and notable factual or terminological details a reader must have.
5. Contextualizes within the work: Note how this section relates to the document's structure, using only what the structure block and this section actually indicate. Do not infer the whole document's shape from one section.
6. Highlights key passages: Quote or closely paraphrase passages that carry core ideas and may be needed for later synthesis, attributing each quotation to its correct source.

Writing quality requirements:
- Write clearly and accessibly. Favor direct, concrete sentences over abstract, jargon-heavy ones.
- Keep sentences reasonably short; break up long, multi-clause constructions.
- Prefer plain wording over ornate vocabulary when the plain word is just as precise. The goal is to inform fellow researchers efficiently, not to impress them.
- Use concrete examples from the text to illustrate abstract points wherever possible.
- Do not add navigation artifacts, links, file paths, or invented headers (such as an author line) that are not part of the section's content.
- Maintain academic rigor and correct terminology, but do not let style obscure meaning.

Write for fellow researchers who need to understand not just what the section says, but how and why—while trusting that everything you report is genuinely in this section, attributed correctly, and complete in its specifics.

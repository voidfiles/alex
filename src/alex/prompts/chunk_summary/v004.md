- What are the core arguments, claims, and conclusions stated in this section?
- What specific evidence, examples, data points, citations, definitions, and named concepts appear? List the concrete particulars, not just the general themes.

- What attributions does the source make (e.g., "X (year) argued/summarized/distinguished...")? Note the precise claim attached to each.

- What assumptions or limitations does the source itself acknowledge?

Two principles govern your summary:

**Completeness of substance.** Capture the specific, salient facts of the section, not just its high-level gist. Preserve named concepts, definitions, enumerated lists, distinctions drawn between terms, specific attributions to authors and years, concrete examples, and any qualifying or hedging language the source uses. If the source enumerates steps, options, or components, reproduce each one. If the source notes that a term was used or avoided in a particular way (e.g., implied rather than stated explicitly), preserve that nuance precisely.

**Strict fidelity to the source.** Summarize only what the section actually states or directly supports. Do not add claims, implications, connections to other theories, or extensions that the source does not itself make. Do not infer that the source "gestures toward," "is compatible with," or "aligns with" ideas it does not explicitly invoke. If you are tempted to draw an inference, include it only if the text licenses it, and mark clearly that it is your interpretation rather than the source's claim. When in doubt, attribute precisely and stay close to the wording.

Now write the summary so that it:

1. **Captures core arguments**: State the central thesis and supporting claims with precision, in the source's own terms.
2. **Records specific details**: Include the definitions, named concepts, enumerated lists, examples, data points, and attributions (author, year, and the exact claim) that appear in the section. Do not generalize away particulars.
3. **Explains evidence and methodology**: Describe how the authors support their claims, citing the specific sources or reasoning they use.
4. **Contextualizes within the work**: Briefly show how this section relates to the document's overall structure, without importing outside material.
5. **Identifies key insights**: Highlight the novel contributions, findings, or distinctions the section actually makes.
6. **Notes acknowledged limitations**: Include any assumptions or limitations the source itself states. Do not invent critiques the source does not raise.
7. **Highlights key passages**: Quote verbatim the passages that best express core ideas and should be retained for later synthesis, clearly marked as quotations.

Writing quality requirements:
- Write clearly and directly. Prefer plain, precise language over ornate phrasing. Avoid jargon-for-its-own-sake constructions (e.g., "schema-theoretic, cognitive-architectural paradigm," "pedagogically-oriented exposition") when a clearer phrase conveys the same meaning.
- Use varied, readable sentence lengths; break up dense sentences, especially when listing limitations or details.
- Use technical terminology where it adds precision, not where it adds only formality.
- Maintain the analytical depth expected of graduate-level discourse, but never let style obscure substance.

Write for fellow researchers who need to understand not just what was said, but how and why—and who will rely on your summary as an accurate, complete stand-in for the source section.

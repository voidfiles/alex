CRITICAL PRINCIPLES — read carefully:

**Completeness of specific substance.** Salient facts are frequently quantitative or otherwise concrete. You must capture every specific detail that carries informational weight, including:
- All numeric results: scores, metrics, percentages, point differences, deltas, rankings, thresholds, sample sizes, counts.
- Named entities: specific systems, datasets, models, methods, variants, baselines, tools, frameworks, and the people/works credited with them.
- Comparative findings: which configuration wins or loses, by how much, and on which measure; any reported costs, runtimes, resource requirements, or trade-offs.
- Exact definitions and the precise scope/conditions under which a claim holds.
Do not abstract away numbers into vague phrases like "roughly" or "improves performance" when the source gives a precise figure. If the source states a value, reproduce that value. Omitting a specific result because it seems minor is an error; treat every concrete data point as potentially essential.

**Strict fidelity to the source.** Summarize only what this section actually states. Do not import outside knowledge, do not name a framework, theory, method, or citation that the section does not name, and do not assert the absence of something (e.g., "no validation was performed") unless the section explicitly says so. If you are inferring or interpreting rather than reporting, mark it clearly as your interpretation. When you are uncertain whether a detail is in the source, either quote it directly or leave it out. Every claim of fact in your summary must be traceable to the section content above.

Now create a PhD-level summary that:

1. Captures core arguments: Identify the central thesis and supporting claims with precision.
2. Analyzes evidence and methodology: Explain how the authors support their arguments, including the specific experimental setups, baselines, and comparisons used.
3. Records all concrete results: Report every specific number, score, metric, cost, runtime, and comparative outcome stated in the section, with its exact value and the condition it applies to.
4. Maintains academic rigor: Use appropriate technical terminology and preserve nuance, while attributing concepts only to the sources the section actually cites.
5. Contextualizes within the work: Show how this section relates to the document's overall structure and argument.
6. Identifies key insights: Highlight novel contributions, significant findings, or important implications as stated by the authors.
7. Notes critical details: Include specific examples, named entities, and definitions that are essential for deep understanding.
8. Highlights key passages: Quote passages that should be retained verbatim for later synthesis.

Writing-quality requirements:
- Favor clarity and scannability. Use short-to-moderate sentences; do not pack multiple distinct findings into a single dense clause.
- Prefer concrete statements of fact over elaborate hedged prose. One idea per sentence where practical.
- Use lists or tables to present multiple numeric results so each figure is easy to locate.
- Keep the analytical depth expected in graduate-level discourse, but write so a fellow researcher can scan and extract specific facts quickly.

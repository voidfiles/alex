## Critical constraints

**Fidelity to the source is paramount.** Summarize only what is actually present in the section content above. Do not import claims from your own background knowledge, do not infer arguments the author does not make, and do not characterize what the document "excludes," "omits," "neglects," or "fails to address" unless the text itself explicitly says so. If you are tempted to note an absence, a critique of another work, a comparison, or a limitation, include it ONLY if the section text directly supports it. When in doubt, leave it out.

**Completeness of substance is equally paramount.** Capture every salient, concrete fact in the section, not just the high-level themes. This explicitly includes:
- Named people, studies, researchers, and the specific findings attributed to them (e.g., a cited researcher's name and what their research showed).
- Named effects, models, frameworks, theories, and their definitions.
- Specific numbers, data points, thresholds, dates, and quantities.
- Concrete examples, analogies, and case studies, including what each is meant to illustrate.
- Direct quotations that articulate a core idea precisely.

Do not let thematic, high-level synthesis crowd out these specific, named, attributable facts. A reader of your summary should not lose any citation-level detail that appears in the source.

## Before writing, think through:
- What are the core arguments and the evidence presented for them?
- Which specific named people, studies, effects, numbers, examples, and quotations appear, and what does each establish?
- How does this section fit within the broader document structure shown above?
- What theoretical frameworks, methodologies, or key terms are introduced and defined here?
- What assumptions or limitations does the text itself explicitly state?
- Which passages are particularly well-articulated and worth retaining verbatim for later synthesis?

## Now write a summary that:

1. **Captures core arguments** with precision: the central claim of the section and the supporting points.
2. **Preserves all specific evidence**: every named study, researcher, effect, framework, statistic, example, and analogy, paired with what it supports. Err on the side of including a concrete detail rather than dropping it.
3. **Maintains accuracy and nuance** using appropriate terminology, while keeping the wording grounded strictly in the source.
4. **Contextualizes within the work** by relating this section to the document's overall structure.
5. **Identifies key insights**: novel contributions, significant findings, or important implications actually stated in the text.
6. **Retains key passages**: a small number of direct quotations that best capture core ideas, marked as quotations.

## Writing-quality requirements:
- Write clear, readable academic prose. Prefer direct sentences over long, clause-heavy ones. Aim for sentences that a fellow researcher can parse on the first read.
- Use technical terminology where it carries real meaning, but do not pile on ornamental jargon. When a precise term is essential, make its sense clear from context.
- Do not repeat the same point across multiple sections. State each idea once, in its most developed form.
- Do not append lists of file references, chunk indices, navigation links, or boilerplate. Produce only the substantive summary.

Write for fellow researchers who need to understand not just what was said, but how and why—while being able to trust that every claim in your summary is genuinely supported by the section above.

Two principles govern your summary. They are equally important.

STRICT FIDELITY TO THE SOURCE. Every claim in your summary must be directly supported by the text of THIS section. Do not infer, extrapolate, characterize, rank, or editorialize beyond what the text states. Specifically:
- Do not invent framing, labels, or value judgments the authors do not use. Do not call something "the most important," "the most interesting," "novel," "circular," "self-referential," "democratizing," or "counterintuitive" unless the text itself applies that characterization. Report the authors' own framing in their own terms.
- Represent novelty claims exactly as scoped. If the text claims novelty for "applying X to task Y," do not widen it to "repurposing X" in general. Match the precise object of the claim.
- Represent comparative claims exactly as worded. If the text says one method "performs worst" or "exhibits the most hallucination," do not restate this as another method "substantially outperforms" it unless that exact comparison is stated. Preserve the direction and strength of every comparison.
- Attribute every number to exactly what it measures. Do not merge two figures into a range, or assign a value from one model, table, or condition to another. Tie each number to its specific source (which model, which table, which metric, which setting).
- Distinguish intended deployment from technical fact. If the text says access "will be" restricted to certain users as an intended use, do not state that dataset access "is" restricted. Keep the modality and scope the text uses.
- Do not make claims about the document as a whole (publication dates, span of the reference list, overall balance of examples, audience, licensing) unless that information is explicitly present in THIS section's content.
- Attribute every citation, definition, and finding to the exact source named in the text, and represent what that source is said to claim precisely—including negative claims (what a source did not state or did not include).
- If something is ambiguous or only implied, either omit it or mark it explicitly as your interpretation rather than asserting it as fact.

COMPLETE AND SPECIFIC COVERAGE. Capture the section's full substance, not just its high-level thrust. A downstream reader will be scored on whether specific, salient facts survive, so do not drop concrete details:
- Named authors and the specific year of each cited work, with the precise claim attributed to each (e.g., who introduced a concept, who released a named benchmark or dataset).
- Exact identifiers: full model names and version strings, hyperparameter settings, dataset names and sizes, token or length limits, cost figures, accuracy or score values, and the table or figure number that reports each.
- Funding sources, supporting institutions, and named tools or organizations credited or licensed in this section.
- Exact definitions, the precise wording of distinctions (e.g., between two named components or concepts), and any enumerated categories, steps, or roles.
- Stated motivations and rationales (e.g., why a dataset was created, why a metric was chosen, what a procedure focuses on).
- Functional details a reader must have, including which elements are optional or conditional (e.g., a prompt passage that is omitted in one configuration).
- Specific examples, numbers, qualifying conditions, and the real-world purposes or use cases the section names.
- If this section is a references list, an appendix of examples, or another non-prose section, say so plainly and capture its salient named contents (e.g., specific benchmarks, datasets, or tables it contains).

Now write a summary that:

1. Captures core arguments: Identify the central claims and supporting points of this section with precision, in the section's own terms.
2. Reports evidence and methodology faithfully: Explain how the authors support their points, naming each source, tool, dataset, model, and number, and the specific claim or measurement each backs.
3. Preserves key definitions and distinctions: Record technical terms, their exact definitions, enumerated steps or roles, and any explicit contrasts, qualifications, or optional elements.
4. Retains salient specifics: Include named authors, years, exact identifiers (model versions, sizes, token limits, costs, scores), table/figure references, funding and licensing details, examples, and notable factual or terminological points a reader must have.
5. Contextualizes within the work: Note how this section relates to the document's structure, using only what the structure and this section actually indicate, and name the kind of section it is.
6. Highlights key passages: Quote or closely paraphrase passages that carry core ideas and may be needed for later synthesis, attributing quotations correctly.

Writing quality requirements:
- Write clearly and accessibly. Favor direct, concrete sentences over abstract, jargon-heavy ones.
- Keep sentences reasonably short; break up long, multi-clause constructions.
- Prefer plain wording over ornate vocabulary when the plain word is just as precise. The goal is to inform fellow researchers efficiently, not to impress them.
- Avoid evaluative filler ("notably," "candidly," "deserves attention," "compelling," "valuable") unless the source itself makes that judgment. Spend words on facts, not on praise.
- Use concrete examples from the text to illustrate abstract points wherever possible.
- Maintain academic rigor and correct terminology, but do not let style obscure meaning.

Write for fellow researchers who need to understand not just what the section says, but how and why—while trusting that everything you report is genuinely in the source and nothing has been added, inflated, or carried in from elsewhere.

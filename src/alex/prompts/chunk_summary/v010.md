Two principles govern your summary. Both are mandatory.

STRICT FIDELITY TO THE SOURCE. Every claim in your summary must be directly and verifiably supported by the text of THIS section. Do not infer, extrapolate, or characterize beyond what the text states. Specifically:
- Do not invent framing the authors do not use (e.g., do not relabel a theory's stance, do not call something an "architecture," "primer," or "evolution" unless the text uses such terms).
- Do not make claims about the document as a whole—publication venue, publication date, the number or span of references, author biographies, audience, licensing, or the balance of examples across domains—unless that exact information is explicitly present in THIS section's content. If this section is not the references section, do not state how many sources the document cites.
- Do not attribute a claim to a source unless THIS section explicitly makes that attribution. Match each finding to the exact source named, with the exact year given. Do not merge, swap, or paraphrase attributions across different cited works. If the text attributes a specific mechanism or wording to one author, do not transfer it to another.
- Do not describe what a section other than this one does (e.g., do not say "the opening section establishes X" or "the conclusion affirms Y") unless THIS section's text states it. Describe only the section in front of you.
- Represent negative claims precisely (what a source did NOT state, what a term was NOT originally called), and only when the text makes them.
- If something is ambiguous or only implied, either omit it or mark it explicitly as an interpretation ("the section appears to imply...") rather than asserting it as fact.
- Before finalizing, re-read each sentence of your summary and confirm you can point to the specific text in this section that supports it. Delete or correct any sentence you cannot ground.

COMPLETE AND SPECIFIC COVERAGE. Capture the section's full substance, not just its high-level thrust. Missing a concrete, salient detail is as serious an error as inventing one. Do not omit:
- Named authors and the specific year of each cited work, paired with the precise claim attributed to each.
- Every item in any enumerated list, set of steps, or set of recommendations. If the section lists concrete practices (for example, specific actions an educator can take), reproduce each one. Do not collapse a multi-item list into a single summary phrase or drop items.
- Exact definitions, the precise wording of distinctions (e.g., between two named components or concepts), and any enumerated categories.
- Fine-grained but important factual points, including historical or terminological observations (e.g., that a particular term was or was not used in an original formulation, and how the idea was instead expressed).
- Specific examples, numbers, and qualifying conditions stated in the text.

Now write a summary that:

1. Captures core arguments: Identify the central claims and supporting points of this section with precision, in the section's own terms.
2. Reports evidence and methodology faithfully: Explain how the authors support their points, naming each source and the specific claim it backs, with correct attribution and year.
3. Preserves key definitions and distinctions: Record technical terms, their exact definitions, and any explicit contrasts or qualifications.
4. Retains salient specifics: Include the named authors, years, examples, and every item of any enumerated list, along with notable factual or terminological details a reader must have.
5. Contextualizes within the work: Note how this section relates to the document's structure, using only what the structure and this section actually indicate, and without describing the internal content of other sections.
6. Highlights key passages: Quote or closely paraphrase passages that carry core ideas and may be needed for later synthesis, attributing quotations correctly.

Writing quality requirements:
- Write clearly and accessibly. Favor direct, concrete sentences over abstract, jargon-heavy ones.
- Keep sentences short. When a sentence carries more than one citation or more than one distinct claim, split it into separate sentences. Do not stack multiple clauses and citations into one dense construction.
- Prefer plain wording over ornate vocabulary when the plain word is just as precise. The goal is to inform fellow researchers efficiently, not to impress them.
- Use concrete examples from the text to illustrate abstract points wherever possible.
- Avoid restating the same structural point (such as a recurring taxonomy) more than once; introduce it clearly and then refer back to it concisely.
- Maintain academic rigor and correct terminology, but do not let style obscure meaning.

Write for fellow researchers who need to understand not just what the section says, but how and why—while trusting that everything you report is genuinely in the source, and that no concrete detail from the source has been dropped.

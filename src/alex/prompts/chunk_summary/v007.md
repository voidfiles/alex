Before writing your summary, think through:
- What are the core arguments and evidence presented?
- How does this section fit within the broader document structure?
- What theoretical frameworks or methodologies are employed?
- What are the key technical terms, definitions, or concepts introduced?
- What specific facts, named sources, dates, qualifications, caveats, and historical or definitional details appear in the text—including ones that are easy to overlook?
- What assumptions or limitations does the source itself explicitly state?
- What key passages explain core ideas or are particularly well-articulated and should be kept in the final summary?

Core principles for this summary:

STRICT FIDELITY TO THE SOURCE. Report only what is actually present in the section content. Do not introduce claims, critiques, comparisons, or "limitations" that the source does not itself state. In particular:
- Do not assert that the document "fails to address," "does not engage," or "omits" something unless the source explicitly acknowledges that gap. The absence of a topic from a section is not evidence that the topic is absent from the broader work or that the authors neglected it.
- Distinguish clearly between what the source claims and any analytical observation you add. If you note a limitation, ground it in the text rather than inferring it from silence.
- Do not invent or embellish authors' affiliations, motivations, or intentions beyond what the text supports.

COMPLETE COVERAGE OF SUBSTANCE. Capture the section's specific factual content, not just its high-level structure. Pay deliberate attention to details that are easy to lose, including:
- Precise definitions and the exact wording of key distinctions.
- Named scholars, publication years, and what each specific source is credited with saying or summarizing (e.g., who introduced a term, who characterized a concept as multidimensional, who confirmed a finding).
- Historical or terminological nuances (e.g., when a term was or was not used in an original formulation, and how it was instead expressed).
- Numerical data, taxonomies, enumerated mechanisms, and qualifying conditions.
Treat each distinct attributed claim and definitional nuance as a fact worth preserving, even when it seems minor.

CLEAR, ACCESSIBLE ACADEMIC PROSE. Write with the analytical depth expected in graduate-level discourse, but prioritize readability. Avoid packing multiple nested qualifications into single compound sentences. Use precise technical terminology where it earns its place, but do not stack jargon; favor shorter sentences when a claim is complex. Eliminate repetition that does not serve a clear reinforcing purpose.

Before writing, work through the following:
- What are the core arguments, claims, and conclusions actually stated in this section?
- What specific evidence, examples, data points, definitions, and citations appear, and exactly what does each one say? Note especially any named researcher or study together with the precise finding attributed to it, and any specific number, threshold, or quantity.

Two principles govern your summary. They are equally important, and you must satisfy both at once.

STRICT FIDELITY TO THE SOURCE. Every claim in your summary must be directly supported by the text of this section. Do not infer, extrapolate, or characterize beyond what the text states. Specifically:
- Do not invent framing the authors do not use (e.g., do not relabel the theory's stance unless the text itself uses that label).
- Do not assert that the work argues "against," "in contrast to," "for," or "in agreement with" any named author, book, or school of thought unless THIS section explicitly names that target and states the relationship. A passing mention of a source is not evidence of a stance toward it.
- Do not make claims about the document as a whole (publication dates, the span of the reference list, the overall balance of examples across domains, audience, licensing, influence, or reception) unless that information is explicitly present in THIS section's content.
- Attribute every citation, definition, and finding to the exact source named in the text, and represent what that source is said to claim precisely—including negative claims (what a source did not state or did not include).
- If something is ambiguous or only implied, either omit it or mark it explicitly as an interpretation rather than asserting it as fact.
- Before you finalize, re-read each sentence and ask: "Is this exact claim, with this exact attribution, present in the section text?" If you cannot point to where the text supports it, delete it or rewrite it.

COMPLETE AND SPECIFIC COVERAGE. Capture the section's full substance, not just its high-level thrust. A summary that is faithful but omits concrete, salient details has failed. Do not omit:
- Every named author or researcher and the specific year (when given), each paired with the precise claim, study, or finding attributed to them. If the section names a researcher and reports what they found, that researcher and finding must appear in your summary.
- Every specific number, threshold, percentage, quantity, or measured relationship stated in the text (e.g., a stated IQ threshold, a count of works, a sample size). Reproduce the number, not a vague paraphrase of it.
- Exact definitions, the precise wording of distinctions (e.g., between two named components or concepts), and any enumerated categories or steps.
- Fine-grained but important factual points, including historical or terminological observations (e.g., that a particular term was or was not used in an original formulation, and how the idea was instead expressed).
- Specific examples and qualifying conditions.

Treat named-finding pairs and concrete numbers as the highest-priority items to preserve. When deciding what to cut for length, cut high-level restatement before you cut a specific attributed finding or a specific number.

Now write a summary that:

1. Captures core arguments: Identify the central claims and supporting points of this section with precision, in the section's own terms.
2. Reports evidence and methodology faithfully: Explain how the authors support their points, naming each source and the specific claim it backs. Make sure no named researcher or reported study from the section is left out.
3. Preserves key definitions and distinctions: Record technical terms, their exact definitions, and any explicit contrasts or qualifications.
4. Retains salient specifics: Include the named authors, years, examples, enumerated lists, exact numbers and thresholds, and notable factual or terminological details a reader must have.
5. Contextualizes within the work: Note how this section relates to the document's structure, using only what the structure and this section actually indicate.
6. Highlights key passages: Quote or closely paraphrase passages that carry core ideas and may be needed for later synthesis, attributing quotations correctly.

Writing quality requirements:
- Write clearly and accessibly. Favor direct, concrete sentences over abstract, jargon-heavy ones.
- Keep sentences short. If a sentence carries more than one main idea, split it into separate sentences.
- Avoid stacking parenthetical asides and nested clauses inside a single sentence. Where you would use parentheses to add a second claim, prefer a new sentence instead.
- Prefer plain wording over ornate vocabulary when the plain word is just as precise. The goal is to inform fellow researchers efficiently, not to impress them.
- Use concrete examples from the text to illustrate abstract points wherever possible.
- Do not contradict yourself. Use the metadata above consistently; for instance, do not call the authorship unknown if the title, metadata, or section text names an author.
- Maintain academic rigor and correct terminology, but do not let style obscure meaning.

Write for fellow researchers who need to understand not just what the section says, but how and why—while trusting that everything you report is genuinely in the source, and that every specific finding and number in the source survives in your summary.

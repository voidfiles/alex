## Grounding rules (read first)

These rules override any impulse toward eloquence or comprehensiveness:

1. **Strict fidelity to the source.** Every claim in your summary must be directly supported by the text in `<section_content>`. Do not add interpretive framing, name theoretical traditions, attribute methodological stances, or characterize what the source omits unless the section itself states or clearly entails it. If you are tempted to describe the work using outside vocabulary (e.g., naming a named theory, a named bias, a disciplinary tradition, a "replication crisis," or an epistemological category), include it ONLY if the section explicitly invokes it. When in doubt, attribute the source's claim in the source's own terms.
2. **No claims about absence.** Do not assert what the document "does not engage," "fails to address," or "leaves underexplored" unless the section itself makes that observation. You are summarizing one section and cannot see the whole work.
3. **Distinguish source from inference.** If you offer any analytical observation of your own (limitations, significance, connections), mark it clearly as your inference (e.g., "This section implies…") rather than presenting it as the author's claim.

## Capture every salient fact

Missed specifics are the most common failure of this task. Before summarizing, inventory the section exhaustively and ensure your summary preserves:

- **Named people, studies, and researchers** (e.g., cited authors, experiments, named effects) together with the specific finding attributed to each.
- **Concrete data points**: numbers, percentages, thresholds, dates, study results, and quantitative claims, stated with their specifics intact.
- **Named concepts, effects, and terms** introduced or invoked, each with its definition or function as given.
- **Specific examples, anecdotes, and illustrations** the author uses to make a point — these often carry the salient fact.
- **Counterintuitive or surprising claims**, including the evidence offered for them.

If the section cites a study or names a researcher, that citation and its finding must appear in your summary. Do not generalize away specifics; a reader of your summary should learn the same concrete facts a reader of the section would.

## Think through the section first

Before writing, work through:
- What are the core arguments and what specific evidence (studies, data, examples) supports each?
- What named people, effects, concepts, numbers, and findings appear, and what is claimed about each?
- How does this section fit within the broader document structure shown above?
- What assumptions or limitations does the section itself acknowledge?
- Which passages are particularly well-articulated and should be quoted verbatim for later synthesis?

## Write the summary

Produce a summary that:

1. **Captures core arguments** — Identify the central claims of this section with precision, in the source's own terms.
2. **Preserves all salient specifics** — Include every named study, researcher, effect, data point, threshold, example, and quotation that carries substantive content (see the inventory rules above).
3. **Analyzes evidence and methodology** — Explain how the authors support their claims, naming the specific evidence used.
4. **Maintains academic rigor** — Use precise terminology and preserve nuance, without importing vocabulary the source does not use.
5. **Contextualizes within the work** — Show how this section relates to the document's overall structure, based on the headers provided.
6. **Highlights key passages** — Quote, verbatim and in quotation marks, the passages that best express core ideas and should be retained for later synthesis.

## Writing quality

The summary must be readable on a single pass:

- Favor clear, well-paced prose over maximal density. Do not pack multiple major concepts into a single sentence; give each its own space.
- Use smooth transitions so the argument flows from foundational claims to evidence to implications.
- Keep inline citations and cross-references unobtrusive; integrate them into the prose rather than stacking parentheticals that interrupt the line of thought.
- Prefer a clean structure (short headed subsections or labeled points) over long undifferentiated blocks.

Write as if for fellow researchers who need to understand not just what was said, but how and why — and who must be able to trust that every fact in your summary is actually in the source.

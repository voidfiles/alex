A critical warning before you begin. Two failure modes are common and must be avoided:

FAILURE MODE 1 — MISSED FACTS. Omitting concrete, specific details that actually appear in this section: named studies, exact numbers, percentages, specific examples, anecdotes, quotations, enumerated lists, defined terms, individual citations with their years, and even seemingly minor or incidental content (including lists of categories, site names, navigation text, or boilerplate that is literally present). FAILURE MODE 2 — UNSUPPORTED CLAIMS. Asserting things the section does not actually say: inventing thesis statements, attributing ideas to scholars not named here, assigning content to chapters or sections you cannot see, inflating or guessing counts (e.g., "200+ sources"), misplacing where a quote or idea appears, or connecting the text to outside theories or authors it never mentions. Two principles govern your summary:

STRICT FIDELITY TO THE SOURCE. Every claim in your summary must be directly supported by the text of THIS section. Do not infer, extrapolate, or characterize beyond what the text states. Specifically:
- Do not invent framing or thesis statements the authors do not use. Do not relabel or restate the section's stance in your own conceptual vocabulary unless the text itself uses that label.
- Do not make claims about the document as a whole (publication dates, the size or span of the reference list, the number of sources, the balance of examples, audience, licensing) unless that information is explicitly present in THIS section's content. Never estimate or round counts you have not actually verified in the visible text.
- Do not attribute content to a chapter, step, or section you cannot see. Only describe where an idea appears if THIS section states it. Do not guess which chapter introduces a concept.
- Do not connect the section to outside theories, frameworks, or authors (e.g., naming a famous paper or scholar the text alludes to but does not cite). If the text does not name it, do not name it.
- Attribute every citation, definition, statistic, and finding to the exact source named in the text, and represent what that source is said to claim precisely—including negative claims (what a source did not state, did not include, or never published) and qualifications (e.g., that a finding is contested, unpublished, or statistically irrelevant).
- If something is ambiguous or only implied, either omit it or mark it explicitly as an interpretation rather than asserting it as fact.

COMPLETE AND SPECIFIC COVERAGE. Capture the section's full substance, not just its high-level thrust. Do not omit concrete, salient details:
- Named authors and the specific year of each cited work, with the precise claim attributed to each.
- Exact definitions, the precise wording of distinctions (e.g., between two named components or concepts), and any enumerated categories, steps, or lists—reproduced in full, not summarized as "several."
- Specific numbers, percentages, durations, quantities, and the exact qualifying conditions attached to them.
- Concrete examples, anecdotes, and illustrations (e.g., a story about an organization solving a problem, a named individual's quoted remark), including the specific details that make them memorable.
- Fine-grained but important factual points, including historical or terminological observations (e.g., that a particular term was or was not used in an original formulation, and how the idea was instead expressed).
- Incidental but literally present content: lists of named categories, specific website or product names, exact titles of cited works (including foreign-language titles), and any boilerplate or framing text that is part of this section.
- Numbered or labeled steps and principles stated in their own terms (e.g., "the first principle is X," "step 9 advises Y"), preserving both the label and its exact content.

Writing quality requirements:

- Keep sentences reasonably short; break up long, multi-clause constructions.

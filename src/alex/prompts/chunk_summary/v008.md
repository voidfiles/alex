Before writing your summary, think through:
- What are the core arguments and the evidence presented for them?
- What specific facts does this section contain—named studies, researchers, dates, statistics, defined effects, tools, named steps or processes, and concrete examples? List these out so none are lost.
- How does this section fit within the broader document structure?
- What theoretical frameworks or methodologies are employed, and are they explicitly stated in the text or only implied?
- What key terms or concepts are defined here?
- What assumptions or limitations does the section itself acknowledge?
- Which exact passages best capture core ideas and should be quoted in the summary?

Now create a summary that meets all of the following standards.

COMPLETENESS — Capture the section's specific substance, not just its high-level themes:
1. Central arguments: State the main claims with precision.
2. Concrete facts: Include every specific, salient detail the section provides—named studies and their authors and dates (e.g., "Mueller and Oppenheimer (2014) found..."), statistics and thresholds, named effects or phenomena and their definitions, named tools or software, numbered or named procedural steps, and concrete examples. Do not drop a fact merely because it is granular; granular facts are often the most important to retain. Prefer naming the specific finding over gesturing at it abstractly.
3. Evidence and methodology: Explain how the authors support each argument and what kind of support it is (empirical study, logical inference, historical example, citation of prior research).
4. Key insights: Highlight novel contributions, significant findings, and important implications.
5. Definitions and key terms: Preserve technical terms with their definitions as given in the text.

FIDELITY — Stay strictly within the source:
6. Attribute every claim to the section. Do not introduce theories, frameworks, thinkers, or terminology that the text does not itself invoke. If you are characterizing the work using your own analytical vocabulary rather than the author's, say so explicitly, and do not assert interpretive labels (e.g., naming a specific theory or mechanism) as if the text stated them.
7. Distinguish what the section explicitly claims from what it merely implies, and from your own inference. Never present an inference as a stated fact.
8. When a passage is central or especially well-articulated, quote it directly and accurately rather than paraphrasing it into something the author did not say.

READABILITY — Write clearly for fellow researchers:
9. Favor clear, direct prose. Vary sentence length and avoid long, clause-heavy sentences that bury the point.
10. Use plain language wherever it serves as well as a technical term. Reserve specialized vocabulary for places where it carries real, necessary meaning, and briefly gloss any heavy term you do use. Erudition should never come at the expense of clarity.
11. Anchor abstract claims with the concrete examples and data the section provides, so the summary is informative rather than merely impressive.

Write for fellow researchers who need to understand not just what was said, but how and why it was argued—and who must be able to recover the section's specific facts from your summary alone.

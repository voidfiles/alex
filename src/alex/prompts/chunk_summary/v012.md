Two principles govern your summary:

STRICT FIDELITY TO THE SOURCE. Every claim in your summary must be directly supported by the text of this section. Do not infer, extrapolate, or characterize beyond what the text states. Specifically:
- Do not invent framing the authors do not use (e.g., do not relabel the theory's stance unless the text itself uses that label).
- Do not characterize the text's purpose, audience, or genre (e.g., that it is an "orientation text," a "gateway text," a "refresher," or aimed at "graduate students") unless the text explicitly says so.
- Do not add meta-commentary about how the text handles its own material—for example, claims that it presents something "without acknowledging" a tension, that a connection is "invoked rather than developed," that one framing is "distinct from earlier conceptions," or that something "reflects an evolution in the author's thinking"—unless the text itself makes that observation. Describing what the text says is in scope; characterizing what the text fails to do or how it positions itself against unstated alternatives is NOT, unless explicitly stated.
- When a source is presented with a particular definition or claim, report exactly that definition or claim. Do not contrast it against earlier or competing versions, or assign it developmental significance, unless the text draws that contrast itself.
- Do not make claims about the document as a whole (publication dates, the span of the reference list, the overall balance of examples across domains, audience, licensing) unless that information is explicitly present in THIS section's content.
- Attribute every citation, definition, and finding to the exact source named in the text, and represent what that source is said to claim precisely—including negative claims (what a source did not state or did not include).
- If something is ambiguous or only implied, either omit it or mark it explicitly as an interpretation rather than asserting it as fact.

COMPLETE AND SPECIFIC COVERAGE. Capture the section's full substance, not just its high-level thrust. Do not omit concrete, salient details. Treat the following as mandatory to capture whenever they appear in the section:
- Every cited work, with its FULL bibliographic detail as given in the text: author name(s), the specific year, AND the title of the work and its journal, book, or venue when these are stated (for example, in a reference list). Do not collapse a reference to just author and year if the text also gives the title and venue.
- The specific claim, conclusion, finding, or guideline attributed to each cited source—stated in enough detail that a reader knows what that source actually contributed, not merely that it was cited.
- Exact definitions, the precise wording of distinctions (e.g., between two named components or concepts), and any enumerated categories or steps.
- Fine-grained but important factual points, including historical or terminological observations (e.g., that a particular term was or was not used in an original formulation, and how the idea was instead expressed).
- Specific examples, numbers, and qualifying conditions.

If this section is a reference list or bibliography, treat each entry as a salient fact: reproduce the author(s), year, title, and venue of each entry rather than summarizing the list in aggregate.

Now write a summary that:

2. Reports evidence and methodology faithfully: Explain how the authors support their points, naming each source with its full bibliographic detail as stated and the specific claim it backs.
3. Preserves key definitions and distinctions: Record technical terms, their exact definitions, and any explicit contrasts or qualifications.
4. Retains salient specifics: Include the named authors, years, work titles, venues, examples, enumerated lists, and notable factual or terminological details a reader must have.
5. Contextualizes within the work: Note how this section relates to the document's structure, using only what the structure and this section actually indicate.
6. Highlights key passages: Quote or closely paraphrase passages that carry core ideas and may be needed for later synthesis, attributing quotations correctly.

Writing quality requirements:
- Write clearly and accessibly. Favor direct, concrete sentences over abstract, jargon-heavy ones.
- Keep sentences reasonably short; break up long, multi-clause constructions.
- Prefer plain wording over ornate vocabulary when the plain word is just as precise. The goal is to inform fellow researchers efficiently, not to impress them.
- Use concrete examples from the text to illustrate abstract points wherever possible.
- Maintain academic rigor and correct terminology, but do not let style obscure meaning.
- Do not make the summary sound more critical, more sophisticated, or more self-aware than the section itself. Match the section's own level of engagement; report what it claims without adding evaluative framing it does not contain.

Write for fellow researchers who need to understand not just what the section says, but how and why—while trusting that everything you report is genuinely in the source, with nothing added beyond it.

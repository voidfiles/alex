Two principles govern your summary.

STRICT FIDELITY TO THE SOURCE. Every claim in your summary must be directly supported by the text of THIS section. Do not infer, extrapolate, characterize, or editorialize beyond what the text states. Specifically:
- Do not invent framing, labels, or characterizations the authors do not use. For example, do not call a contribution "a novel orchestration rather than a new architecture," do not call a step "targeted" or "intelligent," and do not describe a finding as "honest," "candid," "clean," or "commendable" unless the text itself uses such language. Report what the text says, not your appraisal of it.
- Do not attach a general statement to a specific named entity unless the text makes that link. For example, if the text says "current methods rely solely on transcripts" and separately mentions that certain platforms offer summarization, do NOT claim those specific platforms rely solely on transcripts. Keep general claims general and specific claims specific, exactly as the text does.
- Do not make claims about the document as a whole (publication dates, the span of the reference list, the overall balance of examples across domains, audience, licensing) unless that information is explicitly present in THIS section's content.
- Attribute every citation, definition, and finding to the exact source named in the text, and represent what that source is said to claim precisely—including negative claims (what a source did not state or did not include). Preserve the exact year attached to each cited work.
- If something is ambiguous or only implied, either omit it or mark it explicitly as your interpretation rather than asserting it as fact. Do not add critique, speculation about field-wide concerns, or advice to readers unless the section itself raises these points.

COMPLETE AND SPECIFIC COVERAGE. Capture the section's full substance, not just its high-level thrust. A downstream reader is scored on concrete, salient facts, so do not omit them:
- Named authors and the specific year of each cited work, with the precise claim attributed to each. When a dataset, benchmark, model, or method is named (e.g., a dataset and the work that introduced it), record that name and its citation.
- Exact definitions, the precise wording of distinctions (e.g., between two named components or concepts), and any enumerated categories or steps.
- Concrete operational details: dataset names and what they are adapted from, dataset sizes, the exact backbone model and its version string and settings, token limits, evaluation tables referenced by number, annotation procedures, tools used, and the stated reasons for design choices (e.g., why a dataset was created).
- Funding sources, supporting institutions, licensing or compliance statements, privacy-protection steps, and ethics details when they appear in the section.
- The stated purpose or role that a thing serves (e.g., what meeting summaries are used for), when the section spells it out.
- For a references section, identify it as a reference list and name notable cited works (authors, year, and what each is) rather than skipping it.
- Specific examples, numbers, and qualifying conditions.

Writing quality requirements:
- Write clearly and accessibly. Favor direct, concrete sentences over abstract, jargon-heavy ones.
- Keep sentences reasonably short; break up long, multi-clause constructions.
- Prefer plain wording over ornate vocabulary when the plain word is just as precise. The goal is to inform fellow researchers efficiently, not to impress them.
- Use concrete examples from the text to illustrate abstract points wherever possible.
- Maintain academic rigor and correct terminology, but do not let style obscure meaning.
- Do not pad the summary with evaluation, praise, or commentary about the work's quality. Stay a summary, not a review.

Write for fellow researchers who need to understand not just what the section says, but how and why—while trusting that everything you report is genuinely in the source and that no salient specific has been dropped.
